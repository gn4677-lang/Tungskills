# AI Slop Example Gallery

Use this when a checklist label is too abstract and you need concrete bad-vs-better contrast.

## 1. Hero inflation

Bad:

- giant headline with vague promise
- soft gradient background
- floating product mockup with no readable detail
- CTA before the user knows what the product actually does

Why it fails:

- explanation is displaced by spectacle

Better:

- literal headline and subheadline
- one believable product frame with readable detail
- CTA after a concrete understanding of the offer

## 2. Feature-card filler

Bad:

- six cards with short abstract benefits
- icons are doing more work than the copy
- nothing shows the real workflow

Why it fails:

- card quantity imitates completeness without giving product understanding

Better:

- fewer sections
- task flow, proof artifact, or product walkthrough replacing generic feature stacks

## 3. Fake metrics dashboard

Bad:

- spotless charts, random percentages, placeholder data
- numbers do not connect to any user action

Why it fails:

- credibility collapses when the product proof looks synthetic

Better:

- real or honest sample data
- operator-oriented layout: what changed, what needs action, what can be verified

## 4. Chat-shell cargo cult

Bad:

- every product becomes a left sidebar + chat transcript + right inspector
- domain objects are squeezed into chat because the generator defaults there

Why it fails:

- the interaction model is inherited from AI tooling rather than chosen for the task

Better:

- choose the right base model: canvas, table, queue, editor, workflow, or catalog
- use chat only when conversational exchange is the main user job

## 5. Decorative premium veneer

Bad:

- blur, glass, glow, radial gradients, layered shadows
- weak hierarchy under a heavy style treatment

Why it fails:

- decoration is masking the absence of content hierarchy and product signal

Better:

- restrained surface treatment
- contrast and spacing used to support reading order
- decoration kept subordinate to information

## 6. Empty minimalism

Bad:

- short vague copy
- large blank space
- no clear next action or proof

Why it fails:

- reduction removed necessary information, not unnecessary noise

Better:

- concise but literal copy
- enough structure to support the next decision
- proof where the user hesitates

## 7. State fiction

Bad:

- animated loaders with no scope
- success state that looks finished before the system is actually done
- disabled controls with no reason

Why it fails:

- the UI is telling a nicer story than the system truth

Better:

- scoped progress
- recoverable error states
- explicit disabled reasons and next actions

## 8. Accessibility hidden by visual polish

Bad:

- clickable divs styled as buttons
- weak or missing focus
- information conveyed only by color or motion

Why it fails:

- visual plausibility hides interaction failure for keyboard and assistive-tech users

Better:

- semantic controls
- visible focus
- text/state cues that survive reduced motion and no-color assumptions

## 9. Derivative mimicry

Bad:

- a famous app pattern copied into an unrelated domain
- recognizable shell reused because the generator "knows" it

Why it fails:

- familiarity is replacing domain fit

Better:

- borrow only the useful interaction principle
- recompose the surface around the actual product task and trust needs
